Linear Model Explorer: Observed, Predicted, and Residual Change

An interactive simulator using nested linear models to show how prediction, residuals, and the seminar coefficient change when theory-guided covariate blocks are added sequentially.

Nested Models
Predictors enter in theory-driven blocks. Each model builds on the last rather than adding variables arbitrarily or by stepwise search.
Coefficient Path
Watch how the seminar slope and its CI shift as engagement, belonging, and background controls enter. Shrinkage reveals mediation or confounding.
Incremental Fit
ΔR² and F-change tests show whether each block earns its place statistically, separate from whether it belongs theoretically (the DAG's job).
Scenario
A regional university offers an optional first-year seminar aimed at boosting academic engagement and sense of belonging. Participation is voluntary, so students who enroll may already differ in motivation, preparation, and background. This lab asks: after accounting for engagement, belonging, and pre-existing differences, is there still a seminar effect on first-year GPA? Four nested models build the answer one theoretically justified block at a time.

Purpose

  • This lab breaks the habit of judging models by R² alone. A better model must justify its added predictors on theoretical grounds and show meaningful improvement in fit.
  • The seminar coefficient can shrink, grow, or stay stable as blocks enter. Each outcome tells a different causal story about mediation, confounding, or robustness.
  • Reading the DAG alongside the model table keeps theory and statistics connected — a covariate should belong conceptually before asking whether it helps statistically.

How to Use

  1. Choose a preset in the right panel. Each illustrates a different path structure between seminar, engagement, belonging, background, and GPA.
  2. Use the Displayed model control to step through M1 → M4 and watch every panel update together.
  3. Slide sample size up and down. Watch confidence intervals widen and narrow while the coefficient path stays the same shape.
  4. Click any student in the observed vs. predicted scatter to highlight that case in the residual view too.
  5. Click New observed data repeatedly to see sampling variability: same population, different estimates.

What You Gain

  • An intuition for how mediation vs. confounding manifest differently in a coefficient path across nested models.
  • A feel for when incremental R² is meaningful vs. noise — the F-change test and ΔR² bar work together to make this call.
  • Understanding that RMSE and adjusted R² penalize models for added complexity, unlike raw R².
  • Practice reading a DAG: which nodes and paths justify which predictors entering the model at which step.

Full Reference

Core Concepts

Nested linear models build on each other in a pre-specified order justified by theory, not by stepwise search. Each block enters because it is conceptually meaningful.

The seminar coefficient in M1 is a raw association. As covariates enter, it adjusts for overlap with those covariates. Shrinkage reflects mediation or confounding; stability reflects robustness.

RMSE is the residual standard error — √(SSE / (n − k − 1)). It penalizes models that add predictors without proportionately reducing error, making it a fair cross-model comparison metric.

Confidence intervals use a t critical value with df = n − k − 1 rather than the fixed z = 1.96, which matters most at smaller sample sizes where the t-distribution has heavier tails.

Reading the Coefficient Path — and What to Do Next

Slope shrinks after M enters
Engagement mediates the seminar effect. What to do: Report both the raw M1 slope and the adjusted M2+ slope. Consider a formal mediation analysis (e.g., Baron-Kenny or bootstrapped indirect effects) to decompose the total effect into direct and indirect paths. Ask whether the indirect route through engagement is the mechanism your program intends to activate.
Slope shrinks after Z/U enter
Background confounding. Students who join the seminar differ systematically on factors that also predict GPA. What to do: Prefer the M4 adjusted estimate. Assess whether unmeasured confounders remain (sensitivity analysis). Consider propensity score matching, inverse probability weighting, or an instrumental variable if selection is strong enough to cast doubt on the adjusted estimate.
Slope stays stable
The seminar effect is robust to the added covariates. What to do: Report the M4 estimate as your preferred specification. The stability across models strengthens a causal interpretation, though unmeasured confounding can still be present. Transparency about what was and was not controlled for is essential.
Slope grows after controls enter
Suppression. A covariate is blocking a component that was attenuating the raw slope. What to do: Examine which block caused the growth. If background controls suppress, it may mean disadvantaged students disproportionately join the seminar and benefit more — a sign of heterogeneous treatment effects worth exploring by subgroup.

DAG Key

  • X = Seminar participation (focal predictor, binary)
  • M = Course engagement subscale (Q1–Q3 mean; post-X pathway)
  • B = Belonging subscale (Q4–Q6 mean; post-X pathway)
  • Z = ACT z-score (background academic preparation)
  • U = Pell grant and first-gen status (background socioeconomic factors)
  • Y = First-year GPA (outcome, clamped 0–4.0)

Residual Subgroup View

The subgroup box plot shows residual distributions for three groups: first-generation students, Pell-grant recipients who are not first-gen, and students in neither category. In models M1–M3 that do not control for background factors, the box medians for disadvantaged groups will sit below the zero line — the model over-predicts their GPA because it does not account for their disadvantage. When M4 enters background controls (actZ, pell, firstgen), those predictors absorb the systematic gap and the box medians should re-centre near zero. Use the Background confounding preset to see this most clearly: that scenario has the largest background effects and the strongest selection, making the subgroup pattern most visible.

F-Change Tests

The F-change statistic tests whether the increment in R² from adding a block of Δk predictors is larger than expected by chance:

F = (ΔR² / Δk) / ((1 − R²_full) / (n − k_full − 1))

where k_full is the number of predictors in the fuller model. The denominator degrees of freedom are n − k_full − 1. A significant F-change means the increment is unlikely under the null that all added coefficients are zero — but it does not replace the theoretical justification for including the block.

Subscale Reliability (Cronbach's α)

α = (k / (k−1)) × (1 − ΣVar_item / Var_total). It reflects internal consistency — how much the items in a subscale move together. Q1–Q3 (course engagement) and Q4–Q6 (campus belonging) are treated as conceptually distinct constructs following Kuh's engagement framework rather than as six interchangeable items. Higher α supports treating the mean as a reliable composite score.

Simulation Design Notes

  • Seminar participation is assigned via a latent propensity model, so selection into the seminar is non-random and related to background factors.
  • Survey items Q1–Q6 are generated as Likert-style responses (1–5, clamped) with seminar and background factor effects built in.
  • Outcome noise is fixed at 0.35 GPA units. This adds realistic unpredictability without overwhelming the structural effects.
  • Each click of "New observed data" draws a fresh sample from the same data-generating process, illustrating sampling variability of estimates.

Incremental Fit — What Is Worth Adding?

A meaningful block should pass two tests: it should belong to the causal story shown in the DAG, and it should produce a statistically and substantively meaningful increment in fit. A block that is theoretically defensible but adds ΔR² < 0.01 with a non-significant F-change is a marginal addition. A block that adds ΔR² > 0.05 with a significant F-change earns its place in both respects.

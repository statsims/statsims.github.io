The Core Idea

  • A card's value never changes. Only its group label does.
  • The p-value is the fraction of the histogram beyond the observed line, nothing more.
  • Small samples produce a wide null distribution, making even real effects hard to confirm.
  • Significance does not mean importance. A tiny effect can be unusual at large n.

Full Reference

Core Concepts

Permutation test (shuffle test, randomisation test) asks whether the observed difference between groups is unusual under a null world where labels are arbitrary. It builds its reference distribution from the actual data, making it distribution-free.

Null hypothesis: the group label carries no information. Any assignment of values to groups is equally plausible, which justifies random reshuffling.

Fixed values, changing labels. Values never move. On each shuffle only the group assignment changes. This is why cards keep their face values in every step.

p-value approximation: the fraction of shuffles producing a result at least as extreme as observed. With enough shuffles this converges to the exact permutation p-value.

The Five Teaching Scenarios

Null is true
Zero separation. Observed line near centre. p near 1.0.
Small but real
Genuine but uncertain separation. p often 0.10 to 0.30.
Clear separation
Large separation, far tail, unambiguous small p.
Underpowered
Real effect, tiny sample. Wide distribution obscures the result.
Large n, tiny effect
Trivial difference becomes statistically unusual. Significance without importance.

Tail Rules

Two-sided: counts shuffles where |diff| is at least as large as |observed|. Use when no directional hypothesis was committed in advance.

One-sided (greater): counts shuffles where diff is at least as large as observed. Requires pre-commitment. Fails if the true direction is opposite.

One-sided (less): the symmetric counterpart for the other direction.

Reading the Null Distribution

  • Grey bars: differences from random label shuffles. Centred near zero under the null.
  • Red bars: shuffles as extreme as the observed result. Their fraction is the p-value.
  • Black line: the observed difference. In the tail = unusual. In the grey body = consistent with null.
  • Dashed blue lines: reference percentiles from the shuffle distribution, not formal critical values.

When the Test Can Mislead

  • Non-exchangeability: if groups differ in variance or data have dependencies, the null (any assignment is equally likely) fails.
  • Small samples: the p-value cannot go below 1 divided by the number of distinct permutations.
  • Significance without importance: at large n, trivially small differences become unusual. Always assess practical magnitude.
  • Multiple testing: running many comparisons inflates false discovery rates. This test is not immune.

Why 0.05 Is a Convention, Not a Law

Results at p = 0.049 and p = 0.051 carry virtually identical information. The threshold became a default through convention, not mathematical necessity. The real question is always: how unusual is this result, and how large and practically meaningful is the effect?

Effect Size, Significance, and Power Lab

This app separates three ideas students often collapse into one: how large the difference is, whether it is statistically detectable, and how likely the design is to detect it. Draw one sample, then change n, the null value, and the practical threshold to see how the same observed study is re-read under different design choices.

Observed Groups

The blue and red curves summarize the two observed groups. The vertical markers show the group means. When you change n, the app keeps the same sample foundation and only extends or trims it instead of drawing a completely new study.

Decision View: Null, Interval, and Practical Size

The black point is the observed mean difference (Group B mean minus Group A mean). The blue segment is the confidence interval. The dashed gray line is the null value. The green band marks differences you defined as practically small.

Help with Interpretation

Main idea: Statistical significance is about whether the observed result looks incompatible with a null value under the chosen rule. Effect size is about magnitude. Power is about how likely your design is to detect a real effect. Changing from a two-sided rule to a one-sided rule changes the power curve and the rejection logic.

What to compare: Hold the observed sample fixed and increase the sample size. The observed difference may move only modestly, but the interval narrows and power rises because the design becomes more precise.

Practical threshold: The green band marks values you regard as too small to matter much in practice. A result can be statistically significant and still sit close to that practically small region.

Four common cases: statistically detectable but practically small, potentially practically notable but inconclusive, both statistically detectable and practically notable, or little evidence of a notable difference.

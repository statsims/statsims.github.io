Reading the Chart

  • Blue dashed line: The mean. Its label updates live.
  • Green solid line: The median. When it differs from the mean, the data is skewed.
  • Shaded box: The IQR, the middle 50% of the data, from Q1 to Q3.
  • Dashed whiskers: Extend to the minimum and maximum non-outlier values.
  • Blue dots: Typical values, stacked vertically when they overlap.
  • Red dots: Statistical outliers, more than 1.5 times the IQR beyond the box.

Key Takeaways

  • When mean equals median, the data is roughly symmetric and the mean is reliable.
  • When mean exceeds median, a right tail is pulling the average up. Use the median.
  • The IQR and MAD are always the right spread measures when outliers are present.
  • No single number fully describes a bimodal distribution. Always inspect the shape.
  • Standard deviation squares distances, so one outlier can inflate it dramatically. MAD does not.

What is the Mean?

The mean (arithmetic average) is what you get if you spread all values out equally across every observation. It is also the balance point of the data: values above pull right, values below pull left, and they cancel out exactly. This is why the sum of all deviations from the mean is always zero.

x-bar = (x1 + x2 + ... + xn) / n = 1578 / 20 = 78.9

Equal Shares

If you added up all 20 values and split the total equally, each would receive 78.9. That is exactly what the mean represents: the share each observation would get if all were equal.

The Balance Point

The mean is the fulcrum where a number-line seesaw balances. Every value above pulls right, every value below pulls left. They cancel out exactly, which is why the sum of deviations is always zero regardless of the dataset.

When It Works Best

The mean is most informative when data is roughly symmetric with no extreme outliers. It uses every data point, making it statistically efficient, and it is required by most formal tests: t-test, ANOVA, and regression all depend on it.

When It Misleads

One extreme value can make the mean completely unrepresentative. A single billionaire walks into a room and the mean income of everyone inside jumps to hundreds of millions, while the median barely moves.

Full Reference

Measures of Center

Mean is the sum of all values divided by n. It is the balance point of the data: the sum of deviations above and below the mean is always exactly zero. It uses every observation, which makes it statistically efficient but sensitive to extreme values.

Median is the middle value when data is sorted. It depends only on position, not magnitude, which makes it completely resistant to outliers. When data is skewed, the median is almost always a better summary of center than the mean.

Mode is the most frequently occurring value. It is the only measure of center that applies to categorical data. Data can be bimodal or multimodal, and in those cases no single mean or median captures the distribution's shape.

Harmonic mean is defined as n divided by the sum of reciprocals. It is the correct average for rates and ratios (speeds, P/E ratios, efficiencies) where arithmetic averaging overcounts fast values.

x-bar = sum(xi) / n H = n / sum(1/xi)

Measures of Spread

Range (max minus min) is the simplest spread measure but is completely determined by the two most extreme values. A single typo can make it meaningless.

IQR (Q3 minus Q1) is the spread of the middle 50% of the data. It is immune to outliers because it never touches the extremes. Tukey's fence rule uses 1.5 times the IQR beyond Q1 and Q3 to flag outliers.

Standard deviation squares the distances from the mean before averaging them (then square-roots the result). Squaring amplifies large deviations, so outliers have a disproportionate effect. Required by most formal tests.

MAD (Median Absolute Deviation) takes the median of all absolute distances from the median. It is the most robust spread measure available. Multiplied by 1.4826, it estimates the standard deviation for normal data.

s = sqrt(sum((xi - x-bar)^2) / n) MAD = median(|xi - M|)

Key Rules and Thresholds

Outlier fences
Below Q1 minus 1.5 * IQR or above Q3 plus 1.5 * IQR. Red dots on the chart.
Normal distribution rule
68% within ±1s, 95% within ±2s, 99.7% within ±3s of the mean.
Skew signal
Mean above median means a right tail. Mean below median means a left tail.
MAD to sigma
For normally distributed data: sigma is approximately 1.4826 times the MAD.

Robust Alternatives to the Mean

Trimmed mean: Remove the top and bottom p% of values, then take the arithmetic mean of what remains. Olympic judges have used this for decades: drop the highest and lowest scores to reduce the influence of any single judge's extreme rating.

Winsorized mean: Instead of removing the extremes, replace them with the value at the trim boundary, then average. This preserves the sample size while limiting distortion. Preferred over trimming when n is small.

Weighted mean: Assign each observation a weight reflecting its importance, then divide the weighted sum by the total weight. Used in GPA calculations, portfolio returns, opinion polls, and any situation where some observations count more than others.

w-bar = sum(wi * xi) / sum(wi)

Choosing the Right Measure

  • Symmetric, no outliers: Use the mean and standard deviation. They are most efficient and required for t-tests, ANOVA, and regression.
  • Skewed or outlier-prone: Use the median and IQR. Income, home prices, and reaction times nearly always fall here.
  • Rates and ratios: Use the harmonic mean. Arithmetic averaging of rates produces incorrect results.
  • Categorical data: Use the mode. Mean and median require an ordinal structure that categories lack.
  • Bimodal data: Report both modes and show the full distribution. A single summary statistic hides the story.
  • Spread with outliers present: Use IQR or MAD. Standard deviation squares deviations, which amplifies outlier influence.

Common Misunderstandings

  • "Average" usually means mean in news reports, even when median would be more appropriate. Real estate, income, and salary statistics should always be reported as medians.
  • A large standard deviation does not mean bad data. High-variance processes are real; the SD is describing them accurately. The question is whether the spread was expected.
  • Removing outliers is not automatically correct. An outlier may be the most important data point in the dataset. Always investigate before discarding.
  • The mode is not always unique. Bimodal and multimodal data have more than one mode, which itself is useful information about the distribution's structure.

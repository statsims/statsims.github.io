Linear Model Explorer: Observed, Predicted, and Residual Change

This lab uses nested linear models to show how prediction changes when theory-guided covariate blocks are added. The block structure follows a Kuh-style engagement framework: Q1–Q3 form a course engagement subscale, Q4–Q6 form a campus belonging / matriculation subscale, and background factors enter last.

Help with Interpretation

Main idea: A more complete model changes not only the equation but also which outcomes are predicted well, which cases are over- or under-predicted, and how much of the seminar slope remains after related covariates enter.

Why the block order matters: The app does not add predictors arbitrarily. Course engagement enters before belonging, and background controls enter last. That lets students ask whether changes in the seminar coefficient reflect overlap with engagement, overlap with belonging, or background confounding.

Observed vs predicted: The closer the points sit to the y = x line, the closer the model’s predictions are to the observed GPAs.

Residuals: A residual is observed − predicted. Positive residuals mean the model underpredicted the student. Negative residuals mean the model overpredicted the student.

Coefficient path: If the seminar slope shrinks after engagement enters, some of the original seminar association overlaps with engagement differences. If it stays stable, the seminar effect is more independent of the added block.

Uncertainty: The error bars show approximate 95% confidence intervals for the seminar coefficient in each model. A visible change in the point estimate matters more when the intervals move meaningfully, not when the estimate only wiggles inside broad uncertainty.

Theory link: Following Kuh’s engagement logic, Q1–Q3 and Q4–Q6 are not treated as six disconnected items. They are organized into conceptually distinct subscales that can be evaluated and then used as predictors.

Incremental fit: A meaningful block should not be judged only by whether it can be added. Compare the DAG card with the fit-gain panel to ask whether the new block both belongs conceptually and improves fit enough to matter.

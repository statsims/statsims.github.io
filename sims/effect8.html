Effect Size, Significance, and Power Lab

This app separates three ideas students often collapse into one: how large the difference is, whether it is statistically detectable, and how likely the design is to detect it. Draw one sample, then change n, the null value, and the practical threshold to see how the same observed study is re-read under different design choices.

Help with Interpretation

Main idea: Statistical significance is about whether the observed result looks incompatible with a null value under the chosen rule. Effect size is about magnitude. Power is about how likely your design is to detect a real effect.

z vs exact t: The z approximation treats sample SDs as if they were population SDs. The exact Welch t-test uses the t-distribution with estimated degrees of freedom. Both are shown. At n ≥ 30 the difference is minor; at small n the t-test is more accurate.

Null value: The z-statistic, t-statistic, and p-value are all computed relative to the null value you set — not always zero. Moving the null slider changes every displayed test statistic and p-value accordingly.

One-sided CI: For a one-sided test the confidence interval has one open bound (±∞). Only the critical bound matters for the test. The practical region check uses the finite bound.

What to compare: Hold the observed sample fixed and increase n. The observed difference moves modestly, but the interval narrows and power rises because the design becomes more precise.

Practical threshold: The green band marks differences you regard as too small to matter. A result can be statistically significant and still sit inside that practically small region.

Power curve (signed): The x-axis shows the full signed true mean difference from negative to positive. For a one-sided test, power drops toward alpha on the wrong side — an important reminder that one-sided tests commit to a direction.

MDE (minimum detectable effect): The amber vertical line on the power curve marks the smallest |difference| this design can detect with ≥.80 power at the current n, alpha, and test direction.

Four common cases: statistically detectable but practically small; potentially practically notable but inconclusive; both statistically detectable and practically notable; or little evidence of a notable difference.

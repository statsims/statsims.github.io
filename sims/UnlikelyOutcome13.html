Permutation Test Explorer

An interactive lab for understanding how a shuffle test constructs a null world from the observed data itself, and what it means for an outcome to be unusual under that world.

Null World
If group labels do not matter, the same values could have been assigned to either group. Pooling and re-dealing makes this concrete.
Shuffle
Each re-deal keeps values fixed and only changes labels, producing one possible difference under the null.
Null Distribution
Many shuffles build the distribution of differences expected when the null is true. The observed result is measured against this.

Purpose

  • This lab exists to make the null world tangible rather than abstract. You can watch each point keep its value while only its label changes.
  • A p-value is just a fraction of a histogram. This lab lets you build that histogram one shuffle at a time so the number has a picture behind it.
  • The app breaks the habit of treating the observed result as uniquely meaningful. Under the null, results just as extreme would appear by chance at a rate equal to the p-value.
  • Separation and noise together, not either alone, determine how unusual the observed difference is. The teaching scenarios demonstrate each combination.

How to Use

  1. Start with a teaching scenario from the right panel. Each illustrates a classic situation. The null distribution is pre-filled with 1,000 shuffles.
  2. Step through the four stages using the step track above the dot plot. Watch the highlighted diamond keep its position while its colour changes.
  3. On step 3, notice which dots flash large. Those are the points that switched group from the observed assignment, making the relabelling concrete.
  4. After step 4, run more shuffles or reset and build the distribution yourself one deal at a time.
  5. Slide Group size up and down with the same scenario loaded. A result that looked borderline at n = 6 may be clearly unusual at n = 25, even though the true separation did not change.

What You Gain

  • A concrete picture of the null world: not a formula, but a histogram built from the actual observed values.
  • An understanding that the p-value is simply the fraction of the histogram beyond the observed line, nothing more or less.
  • The intuition that noise and separation interact: a large separation in a noisy dataset may be less unusual than a small separation in a tight one.
  • A feel for why small samples are risky: the null distribution is wide and the observed line rarely falls clearly in the tail.
  • The distinction between a result being unusual and a result being important. A very small difference can be unusual; a large difference can be unremarkable if the noise is also large.

Full Reference

Core Concepts

Permutation test (also called a shuffle test or randomisation test) answers: is the observed difference between groups unusual under a null world where group labels are arbitrary? It builds its reference distribution directly from the observed data rather than relying on parametric assumptions. This makes it distribution-free.

Null hypothesis for this test is that the group label carries no information: the same values would have appeared in either group by chance. Under this hypothesis, any assignment of labels to values is equally likely, which justifies shuffling.

p-value approximation is the fraction of shuffles that produced a result at least as extreme as the observed one. With enough shuffles this converges to the exact permutation p-value. A small value means the observed result is rare in the null world, not that the null is definitely false.

Fixed values, changing labels. The key mechanical fact is that values never move. Only labels (group assignments) change on each shuffle. This is why the dot plot positions are identical in every step of the step-through.

The Five Teaching Scenarios

Null is true
No separation exists. The observed line lands in the body of the null distribution. Nearly all shuffles are as extreme or more so. p-value near 1.0.
Small but real
A genuine separation that is hard to confirm. The observed line sits in a moderate tail. The result is plausible but not clearly unusual. p-value often 0.10 to 0.30.
Clear separation
Large separation relative to noise. The observed line falls in the far tail. Very few shuffles match it. p-value near 0 and the verdict is unambiguous.
Underpowered
A real effect, small sample. The null distribution is wide because few points anchor it. A result that would look clearly unusual at n = 25 is unremarkable at n = 6.
Large n, tiny effect
Large sample, negligible true separation. The null distribution is narrow, so even a trivially small difference falls in the tail. Significance without importance.

Tail Rules Explained

Two-sided counts shuffles where the absolute difference is at least as large as the observed absolute difference. Use this when you had no prior reason to expect one group to be higher than the other.

One-sided (greater) counts only shuffles where the signed difference is at least as large as the observed signed difference. Use only when a directional hypothesis was committed to before data collection. This gives a smaller p-value in the hypothesised direction but fails completely if the true direction is opposite.

One-sided (less) is the symmetric counterpart. The reference percentile line shifts to the 5th percentile. The same caution about pre-commitment applies.

Key Controls Explained

  • Slide Group size while holding everything else fixed. This is the lab's core demonstration. The observed separation barely moves but the null distribution narrows and the observed line moves further into the tail. A result that is unremarkable at n = 6 may be clearly extreme at n = 25, because the same labels are now more informative.
  • Observed separation sets the true population difference between groups. Increasing it moves the observed line toward the tail. Decreasing it moves it back toward the centre. Neither change moves the null distribution, only where the observed line lands within it.
  • Reset null and rebuild manually using step 3 and step 4 repeatedly. This gives direct experience of how each shuffle is one draw from the null world and how the histogram takes shape gradually.
  • New observed data redraws a fresh sample from the same population. Use it repeatedly to see sampling variability: the same separation and noise can produce different observed differences and sometimes different verdicts by chance alone.
  • Tail rule changes what counts as extreme. Switching from two-sided to one-sided at the correct direction roughly halves the p-value, but the reference percentile lines shift accordingly.

Reading the Null Distribution

  • The grey histogram shows the distribution of differences produced by random relabelling. Its centre is near zero because, under the null, relabelling is equally likely to produce positive or negative differences.
  • The red bars use the same bins and mark shuffles that are at least as extreme as the observed result. Their count divided by the total is the p-value approximation.
  • The black vertical line marks the observed difference. If it falls in the red region, the result is unusual. If it sits in the grey body, the result is consistent with the null.
  • Dashed blue lines mark reference percentiles (2.5th and 97.5th for two-sided; 5th or 95th for one-sided). These are landmarks from the shuffle distribution, not formal critical values from a parametric table.
  • Stability requires many shuffles. With 100 shuffles, the tail estimate can vary substantially. With 1,000 or more, the p-value approximation stabilises enough to interpret.

When the Test Can Mislead

  • Non-exchangeability. The test assumes that under the null, any assignment of values to groups is equally plausible. If the groups differ in variance or the data have dependencies (e.g. repeated measures), this assumption fails and the test may give misleading p-values.
  • Small samples. With very few observations, the number of distinct permutations is small and the p-value cannot go below 1 divided by the number of permutations. The test has limited resolution.
  • Significance without importance. At large n, trivially small differences become statistically unusual. Always ask whether the separation is large enough to matter in context, independent of whether it is unusual under the null.
  • Multiple testing. Running many comparisons and reporting those with small p-values inflates the false discovery rate. This test is not immune to multiple comparisons problems.

Correlation Explorer

Five guided lessons — each teaches a distinct concept about correlation and when it misleads.

Purpose

  • This lab is built around one core habit to break: reading a correlation number without asking what produced it.
  • Noise, outliers, nonlinear patterns, small samples, and confounding third variables can each create or destroy a correlation independently of whether a real relationship exists.
  • By working through the five lessons in order, you build a complete picture of what r does and doesn't measure.

How to Use

  1. Click a lesson tab — it loads a preset and a plain-language description of the concept.
  2. Read the blue lesson card, then adjust the controls it points you to.
  3. Watch the four stat cells below the plot update live and read the observation panel for a plain-English interpretation of what changed.
  4. Click ↺ New data repeatedly to see how different random samples behave under the same settings.
  5. In Lesson 5, watch both raw r and partial r — when they diverge, a confound is present.

What You Gain

  • An intuition that r measures linear association only — a strong curved pattern can still produce r ≈ 0.
  • A feel for how one outlier can dominate Pearson's r while Spearman's ρ barely moves.
  • The insight that significance (p-value) is about design quality, not the size of the effect — the same r can be significant or not depending purely on n.
  • An understanding of spurious correlation: two variables can be correlated only because a third variable drives both — partial correlation unmasks this.

Full Reference

Pearson's r — What It Measures

Pearson's r is the ratio of the covariance of X and Y to the product of their standard deviations. It ranges from −1 (perfect negative linear relationship) to +1 (perfect positive linear relationship), with 0 meaning no linear relationship. It says nothing about whether the relationship is curved, causal, or practically important.

Common misreadings: r = 0 does not mean "no relationship" — it means no linear relationship. A U-shaped pattern with tight points still produces r ≈ 0.

Spearman's ρ — Why It Works on Any Numeric Data

Spearman's ρ does not require ordinal data. It works by converting any numeric data to ranks first, then computing Pearson's r on those ranks. This makes it robust to outliers (an extreme value becomes rank 1 or rank n, losing its leverage) and capable of detecting any monotone association — consistently rising or falling — whether curved or straight.

When Pearson and Spearman agree closely, the pattern is approximately linear and robust. When they diverge substantially, there is likely an outlier or a nonlinear pattern distorting Pearson's r.

R² and Practical Significance

R² = r². It is the proportion of variance in Y linearly explained by X. Because r is squared, the scale is highly nonlinear: r = 0.3 explains only 9% of variance; r = 0.7 explains 49%; r = 0.9 explains 81%. A "statistically significant" r = 0.15 at large n explains only 2.25% of variance — detectable but rarely practically meaningful.

Partial Correlation

Partial r(X,Y|Z) is the correlation between X and Y after removing the linear influence of Z from both variables. It is computed by regressing both X and Y on Z, taking the residuals from each regression, and correlating those residuals.

The formula is: rXY·Z = (rXY − rXZ·rYZ) / √[(1−rXZ²)(1−rYZ²)]

When to use it: whenever you suspect a third variable Z may be creating or inflating the X–Y correlation. Classic example: ice cream sales and drowning rates are correlated — but both are caused by hot weather (Z). Partial correlation controlling for temperature would reveal the direct X–Y link is near zero.

p-value and Sample Size

The p-value for r is computed via Fisher's z-transformation: z = atanh(r) · √(n−3), then compared to the standard normal. The critical insight is that p depends on both r and n simultaneously. You can achieve p < .001 with a trivial r = 0.10 if n is large enough. Conversely, a strong r = 0.5 will be non-significant at n = 10.

Statistical significance answers "is this different from zero given the design?" It does not answer "is this large enough to matter?"

The Four Interpretation Cases

✓ Strong + Significant
Large |r|, p < .05. The pattern is both real and substantial. Most reliable verdict.
⚠ Significant, Weak
Small |r|, p < .05, large n. Detectable but R² is small — practical importance is limited.
⚠ Promising, Inconclusive
Moderate |r|, p > .05, small n. The effect may be real but the design is underpowered to confirm it.
✗ Neither
Near-zero |r|, p > .05. Genuine null result — or a curved/outlier-driven pattern that r is failing to capture. Always check the plot.

Why Not Cronbach's Alpha Here?

Cronbach's α measures scale reliability — whether a set of items (e.g., five survey questions) are internally consistent enough to be summed into a single score. Its formula involves the average inter-item correlation across all item pairs, but the underlying question is completely different from bivariate or partial correlation. It belongs in a psychometrics or scale-construction lab, not here.

When to Use Spearman Instead of Pearson

  • Outliers are present and you want a robust measure.
  • The pattern looks curved but consistently increasing or decreasing.
  • Normality is doubtful and you prefer a nonparametric approach.
  • When Pearson and Spearman agree closely, either is defensible and Pearson is conventionally preferred for its interpretability.

Lesson 1 — Noise & Scatter

Core idea: Pearson's r measures how tightly points cluster around a straight line. Noise (vertical scatter) weakens that clustering — and therefore weakens r — even when the direction of the trend is perfectly obvious to the eye.

Try this: Drag the Noise slider all the way to 0. The points fall on a near-perfect line and r approaches ±1. Now slowly increase noise and watch r fall. Notice the regression line barely changes direction — the trend is still real. Only the precision of r is affected. This is why you should always report a scatterplot alongside a correlation: the number alone doesn't tell you how obvious the pattern looks.

Why Spearman here?

Spearman's ρ doesn't require ordinal data. It converts any numeric values to ranks first, then computes correlation on those ranks. Two results follow:

Outlier robustness
An extreme value becomes rank 1 or n. It can't drag the coefficient the way it drags Pearson's r.
Monotone detection
Any consistently rising or falling trend — curved or straight — gets a high ρ. Pearson only sees the straight-line part.
Activate an outlier or curved pattern to see Pearson and Spearman diverge.

Confidence Interval Explorer

Explore confidence intervals as repeated-sampling procedures. The app emphasizes long-run coverage, interval width, and how assumptions shape performance.

Coverage
A confidence procedure is judged by how often intervals capture the true mean across repeated samples, not by any single interval.
Precision
Wider intervals are safer but less precise. Sample size and confidence level directly determine how wide each interval must be.
Assumptions
Skewed populations and small samples can move observed coverage away from the nominal target. The z and t methods make different demands.

Purpose

  • A confidence interval is a procedure, not a probability statement about one fixed result. The question is whether the procedure captures the true mean at the advertised rate over many repeated samples.
  • This app makes that abstract idea concrete by letting you watch many intervals being built, colored by whether they capture the true mean.
  • The key insight is that the true mean is fixed; the interval varies from sample to sample. Approximately the right fraction should be green.

How to Use

  1. Load a preset to start from a canonical teaching case.
  2. Click Draw new intervals several times. Watch coverage fluctuate around the nominal level. Cumulative coverage stabilizes as runs accumulate.
  3. Increase n to see intervals narrow without changing the coverage rate much.
  4. Increase the confidence level to see intervals widen. The coverage rate rises toward the new nominal level.
  5. Switch to a skewed population with small n to see why the normal approximation assumption matters for coverage.
  6. Compare the sample SD with z method against the sample SD with t method at small n to see where z undercounts uncertainty.

What You Gain

  • A clear intuition that confidence refers to the procedure, not to your belief about where the true mean lies after seeing one interval.
  • An understanding that wider intervals are not a failure -- they reflect honest uncertainty, and the right width depends on the design, not the data alone.
  • A feel for when the t method matters: at large n the z and t results converge, but at small n the t method gives more conservative and more accurate coverage.
  • The ability to diagnose undercoverage: small n and skewed populations are the main stress cases for the standard interval formula.
  • An intuition for cumulative vs. single-run coverage: any one run may look off-target, but the long-run rate stabilizes near the nominal level for a well-specified procedure.

Full Reference

Core idea

A confidence interval is a procedure, not a probability statement about one fixed result. Before the sample is drawn, there is a 95% probability (under a 95% procedure) that the resulting interval will capture the true mean. After the interval is computed, the true mean is either inside it or not -- probability no longer applies to that specific interval.

The app visualizes this by drawing many intervals and coloring them by outcome. Roughly the right fraction should be green across a large number of runs.

Four coverage scenarios

Near-nominal coverage
Observed rate is close to the nominal level. The procedure is working as advertised. Some misses are expected and normal.
Undercoverage
Observed rate is meaningfully below nominal. Common causes: sample SD with z critical at small n, or skewed population where the normal approximation breaks down.
Overcoverage
Observed rate is above nominal. Wider-than-necessary intervals (e.g., using t when z would do at large n) produce conservative coverage at the cost of precision.
Sampling variation
Any single run can deviate from the nominal level by chance. Cumulative coverage across many runs reveals the true long-run rate of the procedure.

How to use this app

  • Fix a population and click Draw new intervals repeatedly to see coverage fluctuate around the nominal level.
  • Increase n to see intervals narrow. The coverage rate should stay near nominal; only the width changes.
  • Increase the confidence level to see intervals widen. A 99% interval is wider than a 95% interval for the same data.
  • Use a skewed population with small n to see why assumptions matter. The sample SD with z method will often show undercoverage here.
  • Compare sample SD with z vs sample SD with t at small n to see where the extra uncertainty in estimating the SD should be accounted for.

The three interval methods

  • Sample SD with t critical (recommended): Estimates the SD from the sample and uses a t critical value that accounts for the extra uncertainty in that estimate. This is the textbook correct method for unknown population SD. The t critical value is larger than z at small n and converges to z as n grows.
  • Sample SD with z critical: Estimates the SD from the sample but uses the z critical value as if the SD were known. This undercounts uncertainty and produces intervals that are too narrow, leading to undercoverage at small n. Useful for demonstrating what goes wrong when the t adjustment is ignored.
  • Population SD with z critical (known sigma): Uses the actual population SD rather than an estimate. This is the theoretically ideal case -- intervals are centered correctly and have the right width -- but it is rarely achievable in practice since the population SD is unknown.

Cumulative coverage

Any single batch of 25 intervals has high sampling variability in its coverage rate. With 25 intervals at 95% nominal, the standard deviation of the observed coverage rate is about 4.4 percentage points, so values from 86% to 100% are all plausible by chance alone. The cumulative coverage row accumulates across all Draw new intervals clicks for the current population and gives a more reliable estimate of the true long-run rate. It resets when you generate a new population or load a preset.

Interpreting misses

  • Missed intervals are not errors in reasoning -- they are expected outcomes of the procedure.
  • Misses tend to be shorter than captures because they came from samples that underestimated the spread.
  • If the miss rate is consistently higher than expected across many cumulative runs, check whether the method assumptions are violated for the current population and sample size.

Interpretation reminders

  • Nominal coverage is the target; observed coverage varies from run to run and approaches nominal only in the long run.
  • Wider intervals often improve coverage but reduce precision. Neither goal dominates -- the right balance depends on the research context.
  • Changing display options does not change the underlying procedure or the intervals already computed.
  • The coverage tolerance shown in the interpretation panel scales with the number of intervals drawn so that the verdict is not misleadingly strict for small batches.

Effect Size, Significance & Power Lab

An interactive simulator for understanding how the size of a difference, the statistical decision rule, and the study design each contribute to what you observe and what you can and cannot conclude.

Effect Size
How large is the difference between groups in meaningful units? Large enough to care about?
Significance
Is the observed difference incompatible with the null hypothesis under your decision rule?
Power
How likely is this design to detect the effect if it truly exists at the assumed magnitude?

Purpose

  • This lab exists to break the common habit of treating statistical significance as the only outcome that matters.
  • A p-value below .05 does not tell you the effect is large, important, or that your design was well-powered.
  • A p-value above .05 does not mean the effect is absent but it may just mean your study was too small to detect it.
  • By letting you see all three concepts simultaneously on the same simulated data, the lab makes these distinctions concrete and testable rather than abstract.

How to Use

  1. Start with a preset on the right panel. Each illustrates a classic scenario.
  2. Observe the four panels: group distributions, the decision plot, the design read, and the power curve.
  3. Slide the group size (n) up and down. Watch the confidence interval narrow and power rise with the observed difference staying roughly the same.
  4. Move the null value to see how testing against a non-zero benchmark changes the z-statistic and p-value.
  5. Adjust the practical threshold (the green band) to define what counts as a meaningful difference for your context.
  6. Click New observed data repeatedly to see how estimates vary across samples from the same population.

What You Gain

  • Separate intuitions for magnitude (Cohen's d and raw difference), inferential decision (p-value and CI), and design quality (power and MDE).
  • An understanding that the same observed data can be "significant" or not depending purely on n, not because the effect changed.
  • A feel for why one-sided tests commit to a direction at a real cost: power falls to the alpha floor on the wrong side.
  • A practical tool for prospective design decisions: use the MDE and n-for-power readout to plan studies before you collect data.
  • The difference between z-approximation and exact Welch t and why it matters at small sample sizes.

Full Reference

Core Concepts

Statistical significance answers: is this result incompatible with the null value under the chosen decision rule (alpha level and test direction)? It depends on the observed difference, the variability, the sample size, and the null. It says nothing about practical importance.

Effect size answers: how large is the difference in meaningful units? Cohen's d expresses the raw difference in pooled standard deviation units. Conventional labels (small ≈ 0.2, medium ≈ 0.5, large ≈ 0.8) are rough guides. Domain context should always inform what counts as practically important.

Power answers: if the true effect is this large, how often would this design produce a statistically significant result? Power depends on n, alpha, SD, and the assumed true effect. Low power means an underpowered study or a real effect will often go undetected.

MDE (Minimum Detectable Effect) is the smallest effect that reaches .80 power at the current n and alpha. It is useful for planning: if your MDE is larger than the smallest effect you care about, you need more participants.

The Four Study Outcomes

✓ Significant + Practical
CI excludes the null AND lies outside the practically small region. The strongest verdict: the effect is real and matters.
⚠ Significant, not Practical
CI excludes the null but part of it still overlaps the practically small region. Detectable but the magnitude is uncertain. Common with large n and small effects.
⚠ Practical estimate, Inconclusive
Point estimate looks notable but CI still spans the null. The classic underpowered scenario. The effect may be real but the design cannot confirm it.
✗ Neither
CI spans the null and even plausible effects remain practically small. Genuine null result, or possibly an underpowered study with a small true effect.

Key Controls Explained

  • Slide n while holding everything else fixed. This is the lab's core demonstration. The means barely move; the CI shrinks and power climbs. A result that was inconclusive at n = 16 may be clearly significant at n = 80 or the same effect, different design.
  • Move the null value away from zero to test against a non-zero benchmark. All z, t, and p statistics update to reflect the new null. The CI is always centered on the observed difference; whether it contains the null depends on where you placed it.
  • Adjust the practical threshold to see how the same interval can shift between "notable" and "trivially small" depending on your domain's standard for what matters.
  • Switch to a one-sided test and watch the power curve. Power increases in the hypothesized direction but falls to alpha on the other side, that is one-sided tests are not free power.
  • Click New observed data repeatedly at a fixed n to see sampling variability: how the same population produces different observed differences and sometimes different verdicts by chance alone.

z Approximation vs Exact Welch t

Both test statistics use the same numerator: (observed difference − null value) / SE. They differ in how the p-value is computed. The z approximation uses the standard normal distribution. The exact Welch t-test uses the t-distribution with Welch–Satterthwaite degrees of freedom, which accounts for the additional uncertainty in estimating the SD from a small sample. At n ≥ 30 the difference is negligible. At n = 10–15 the exact t gives a larger (more conservative) p-value, which is more appropriate.

One-Sided Confidence Intervals

For a one-sided "greater" test the CI is [lower bound, +∞). Only the lower bound is informative where the null is excluded when it falls below that bound. For a one-sided "less" test it is (−∞, upper bound]. The app displays the open bound as ±∞ and uses the finite bound for all practical region checks.

Design Planning Workflow

  • Decide the smallest difference that would matter in your domain (practical threshold).
  • Set the true mean difference slider to that value.
  • Read the n-for-power recommendation. That is the minimum n to reach .80 power.
  • Alternatively, read the MDE at your intended n. If MDE > your smallest meaningful difference, the design is underpowered for that question.
  • Tighten alpha to .01 for high-stakes decisions and observe how n requirements grow.
